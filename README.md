# PolytopiaGuide
A guide for the mobile game *The Battle for Polytopia*, formatted as a technical manual.

# Get started 

*The Battle for Polytopia* is an award-winning turn-based strategy game. 

*Polytopia* is a ‘4X’ (Explore, Expand, Exploit, Exterminate) type game, similar to the *Civilization* series, albeit with much more streamlined and fast-paced game mechanics and a brightly-coloured, low-polygon art style. 

Versions of *Polytopia* are available for iOS, Android, Windows, Mac, and Linux (via Steam), and Nintendo Switch. 

This unofficial guide uses screenshots from the Android and Switch versions of the game, but the strategies and tactics discussed here can be applied to any version. 

This guide has been written with beginners in mind. It explains the core concepts of *Polytopia*, from how to interact with the game, to a complete explanation of the combat mechanic.

## Select a game mode

There are five game modes in Polytopia:

### Single Player

* **Perfection** – 30 turn limit, most points wins.

* **Domination** – No turn limit, capture all cities. 

* **Creative** – Custom map mode, play for points, territory – or play forever.

### Multiplayer

* **Glory** – No turn limit, first player to amass a set number of points wins. 

* **Might** – Capture all capital cities to win. No score limit. 

#### Perfection

**Perfection** mode games are limited to 30 turns and take place on 256-tile maps. The winner is whoever has earned the most points after 30 turns. 

Points are obtained by taking over cities, upgrading cities, building monuments and temples, expanding your city’s population and borders, defeating enemy units, and exploring sections of the map – see the section Point scoring explained for a breakdown of how points are earned. 

#### Domination

**Domination** mode games are not turn-limited. They continue until one player controls every city on the map. Map sizes range from 121 tiles to 324 tiles in size. 

Points are accrued throughout Domination games, but they have no bearing on victory.

#### Creative

**Creative** mode lets you customise the standard game settings, play on maps as large as 900 tiles, play on all-land maps, a big landmass dotted with lakes, a pangea, continents, archipelagoes, or on sea maps dotted with small islands. 

You can play Perfection or Domination-mode games, or play forever on **Infinity**-mode games, which have no score or turn limit – you decide when the game ends. 

Creative games let you play against up to 15 computer-controlled opponents, or play against no-one, if you want to peacefully explore the map, and build up your cities without interference. 

#### Glory

**Glory** games are won by the first player to score a certain amount of points. Point thresholds for victory can be set at the following levels:

* 5,000
* 10,000
* 15,000
* 20,000
* 25,000

Glory games are similar to Perfection games in that the object of the game is to gather points, not conquer enemy tribes – although taking over enemy territory will earn you points, making victory easier. 

#### Might

**Might** games are won by capturing the capital cities of all enemy tribes, and holding them for at least one turn. There are no turn limits or point limits. 

Might games are similar to Domination games in that the object of the game is conquest, the only real difference being you only have to capture and hold the capital cities.

## Tribes 

Once you have selected a game mode, it’s time to pick your tribe.

In *Polytopia*, you can choose from one of the four starting tribes. More tribes can be unlocked with in-game purchases. 

The four free regular tribes on Polytopia are:

* Xin-xi.
* Imperius.
* Bardur.
* Oumaji.

#### Xin-xi

An East Asia-themed tribe, which starts out in a grassy, temperate biome. Xin-xi cities feature pagoda-shaped buildings, and many of their units have kabuto-style helmets. While you can’t build Mines on your first turn, playing as Xin-xi gives you the advantage of being able to see where any nearby Metal Deposits are, which allows you to start planning your economy from the first turn. The default Xin-xi colour is red.

* Starting unit: **Warrior**.
* Starting number of Stars: **7**.
* Starting technology: **Climbing** – allows units to scale **Mountains**, reveals **Metal Deposits** (mining Metal Deposits requires the **Mining** technology).

#### Imperius

A Greco-Roman-themed tribe, also based in a temperate biome. Porticos and temples in the Classical style dot their cities, and their units wear plumed helmets, similar to those of Roman centurions and Greek hoplites. Imperius players have the advantage of being able to upgrade their capital city on the first turn. The default Imperius colour is blue.

Starting unit: **Warrior**.
Starting number of Stars: **5**.
Starting technology: **Organisation** – allows cities to harvest **Fruit** (grow city population by one), reveals **Crops** that can be turned into **Farms** (building Farms requires the **Farming** technology).

#### Bardur

A Norse-Germanic themed tribe, based in a snowy Alpine biome. Bardur structures are rough-looking, wattle-and-daub-type fortresses, and their units wear horned helmets of the stereotypical Viking style. As with Imperius, Bardur players can grow their capital city’s population on the first turn. The default Bardur colour is black.

Starting unit: **Warrior**..
Starting number of Stars: **5**.
Starting technology: **Hunting** – allows cities to hunt **Wild Animals** (grow city population by one).

#### Oumaji

An Arab-Egyptian-style tribe, who start out in a desert. Their buildings are crowned with minarets and resemble clay and wood beam structures from north Africa. Wearing turbans and starting with a fast-moving Rider instead of the typical Warrior, Oumaji have a speed advantage in the early stages of games. The default Oumaji colour is yellow.

Starting unit: **Rider**.
Starting number of Stars: **6**.
Starting technology: **Riding** (allows you to train **Rider** units).

The other regular tribes on Polytopia aren’t available with free versions of the game – they need to be unlocked with in-game purchases. They are:

* Kickoo.
* Hoodrick.
* Luxidoor.
* Vengir.
* Zebasi. 
* Ai-Mo.
* Quetzali.
* Yădakk.

#### Kickoo

A Polynesia-themed tribe, who start out in a grassland biome, always close to the sea. Kickoo buildings are fashioned from wood and are thatched with banana leaves. Always starting next to two Water tiles – even on Dryland (‘Dryland’) maps – Kickoo players can easily upgrade their capital city, and start building navies before most players have even researched Fishing. The default Kickoo colour is lime green.

Starting unit: **Warrior**.
Starting number of Stars: **5**.
Starting technology: **Fishing** – allows cities to catch **Fish** (grow city population by one) and build **Ports** (grow city population by one, allow land units to embark).

#### Hoodrick

A vaguely Robin Hood-themed tribe whose units wear feathered caps, Hoodrick tribes start out in heavily forested sections of the map and have Archery as their starting technology. Naturally, their starting unit is an Archer instead of the usual Warrior. The default Hoodrick colour is brown.

Starting unit: **Archer**
Starting number of Stars: **7**
Starting technology: **Archery** – allows cities to train **Archers**, provides a defence bonus in **Forest** tiles

#### Luxidoor

A South Asia-themed tribe who start off in a bigger capital city that’s already been upgraded to Level 3 – although there are none of the usual upgrades like a Workshop or City Walls present, and you only get two Stars to begin with. Luxidoor players also do not benefit from a starting technology, although you will be able to research one on your second turn, and expand your capital to Level 4 very quickly. Luxidoor units wear turbans and long flowing robes. The default Luxidoor colour is purple.

Starting unit: **Warrior**
Starting number of Stars: **2**
Starting technology: None

#### Vengir

A fantasy-themed tribe who inhabit a blasted Mordor/Morrowind-esque wasteland, Vengir tribes start with a Swordsman and access to Smithery, making them a dangerous tribe for other players to encounter early on, although it’ll be a while before you can build Forges – or much else. The default Vengir colour is white.

Starting unit: **Swordsman**
Starting number of Stars: **5**
Starting technology: Smithery – allows cities to train **Swordsmen** and build **Forges** (two Population points per adjacent Mine)

#### Zebasi 

Zebasi are a desert-based tribe who start with access to the Farming technology, proximity to Fields with Crops, and enough Stars to build a Farm, and therefore can upgrade their capital city on the first turn. As well as a great head start, Zebasi players tend to have room to expand, despite not having access to any strong units for the first few turns at least. Zebasi buildings resemble Sudano-Sahelian architecture, and the default Zebasi colour is orange. 

Starting unit: **Warrior**
Starting number of Stars: **5**
Starting technology: **Farming** – allows cities to build **Farms** (two Population points, can only be built on tiles with **Crops**)

#### Ai-Mo

A Himalayan-Andean themed tribe, Ai-Mo starts out with the Philosophy technology – which means cheaper access to technology on the first turn – and they ride giant llamas into battle. Ai-Mo also starts out with a Mind Bender, so you can convert any enemies who get too close, and heal your own units right from the start. The default Ai-Mo colour is turquoise. 

Starting unit: **Mind Bender** 
Starting number of Stars: **5**
Starting technology: **Philosophy** – allows cities to train **Mind Benders**, triggers conditions for the **Tower of Wisdom** monument.

#### Quetzali

Quetzali is a Mesoamerican-themed tribe who build stepped pyramid-shaped monuments, wear bird-shaped helmets, and use ostrich-type birds for their mounted units. Starting with the Strategy technology, Quetzali is well protected during the vital early stages of the game, can easily forge alliances via Peace Treaties, and begin with a larger-than-average amount of Stars. The default Quetzali colour is jade green.  

Starting unit: **Defender**
Starting number of Stars: **7**
Starting technology: **Strategy** – Allows cities to train **Defenders** and send **Peace Treaties** to rival tribes

#### Yădakk

Yădakk is an Anatolia-themed tribe who wear rich red robes and have access to the Roads technology from the start. Yădakk players also begin with a large amount of Stars, so they can get exploring and building trade networks early on, giving players the potential to upgrade their capital to Level 2 without having to extract any resources. The default Yădakk colour is maroon.  

Starting unit: **Warrior**
Starting number of Stars: **7**
Starting technology: **Roads** – Allows cities to build **Bridges** and **Roads**

> **Note:** Stars is the in-game currency in *Polytopia* – read the [Starting currency](#starting-currency) and Stars Per Turn (SPT) sections for a full explanation.

## Choose the number of opponents

After picking your tribe, you will be asked to choose your opponents as well as choose which type of map you want to play on.

In single player games against computer-controlled opponents, you will be asked to pick how many opponents you want to play against, between **1–15**. 

In multiplayer games, you can either create a local **Pass & Play** type game, where you take a turn and then share your mobile device or Nintendo Switch with friends, or organise an **Online** game with friends you’ve added, or with random players via a lobby.

The maximum number of players supported at any one time is **16**.

## Choose the difficulty level

In single-player mode, pick from one of four difficulty levels – **Easy**, **Normal**, **Hard**, **Crazy**.

The difficulty level determines the aggression of computer-controlled opponents. On Easy and Normal difficulty, tribes tend to not attack you unless provoked, whereas on Hard and Crazy difficulty, you will often be attacked without warning. 

The difficulty level does **not** determine the amount of damage that enemy units can deal and receive – read the Combat section for a breakdown of the damage formulas.

## Pick your opponents

You can’t manually select which tribes you play against in single player games – *Polytopia* will randomly select which tribes you play against when you press ‘Start Game’.

You can however influence who you play against by enabling and disabling tribes from the ‘Pick Your Tribe’ menu. 

Select a tribe, and click the ‘Disable/Enable’ toggle in the top-right corner: 

For example, if you only want to play against Xin-xi and Imperius, you could disable all other tribes. If you wanted to play on an all-desert map, you could enable Oumaji and Zebasi, and no other tribes. 

In multiplayer games against human players, you will be asked to pick both the size of the map and the type of map you want to play on, and there are no restrictions on which tribes can be selected.

## Start the game

Your *Polytopia* game will start with a small portion of the map visible to you, with the rest covered by fog-of-war.

From the first turn, you have at your disposal:

* A **starting unit**. 
* A small number of **Stars**. 
* Your **starting technology**. 
* Your **capital city**, with a 3x3 **border**.

### Take a look around

Before you make your first move with your starting unit, take a look at your surroundings. 

Zoom out of the map to get a better idea of where you are in relation to everything, zoom back in. 

Click on a tile to learn more about the terrain. 

In the screengrab above, you can see that the game tells you that you need to research **Climbing** in order to be able to move units on to **Mountain** tiles. 

Click on the tiles with **resources**. If the resource is available for you to extract, the game will render the resource with a glowing blue outline. 

Resources can only be extracted, mined, or used if they’re within your city’s borders. In the screengrab above, only some of the **Fruit** is rendered with a glowing blue outline – read How to grow your city’s Population for information on border expansion. 

If there are resources nearby but you can’t currently extract them, the dialog box will tell you which technology you need to research in order to do so.

Make a note of the terrain, which resources are close by, and where the edges of the map are. Are you on the coast, or further inland?

While you can’t see the whole of the map straight away, you can use what information you have to start thinking strategically. 

Zooming out all the way lets you find out where the corners of the map are in relation to your capital, and where danger might come from.

### Move your starting unit 

To move your starting unit, click on the unit. 

In most cases your starting unit will be a **Warrior**.

The tiles highlighted by the blue circles indicate where this unit can move to. 

When you know where you want your unit to move, click on that tile to complete the move. 

Most units can move one tile per turn. Others, like **Riders**, **Knights**, and **seagoing units**, can move several tiles in one turn – check the Move statistics of all units here.  


#### Removing fog-of-war

As units move across the map, they remove fog-of-war. 

Most units have a standard **sight range** of **3x3** tiles, which means they can see into tiles immediately adjacent to them, removing the fog-of-war as they move. 

If you move a unit on to a **Mountain** tile, their sight range is extended to **5x5** – double the range. 

The **Scout** naval unit and the **Cloak** land/naval unit have sight ranges of 5x5 as standard. **Cloaks** do not get to extend their sight range any further, even if they are moved onto **Mountain** tiles. 

### Actions after moving your first unit

You now have some choices to make:


1. Train another unit.
2. Extract resources.
3. Research a new technology.
4. Save your Stars.


#### Train another unit

Click on your capital city. This pulls up a list of units which are available to train. 

Training units costs Stars – read the Lists of units section for a full breakdown of unit costs.

Unless you have a starting technology like **Riding**, you will most likely only be able to train another Warrior on your first turn. 

When you’ve trained a unit, it will appear on the city tile and can be moved on the following turn. 

You cannot train a unit in a city if there’s another unit occupying the city tile. 


#### Extract resources

Some **resources** – like **Fruit**, **Fish**, or **Wild Animals** – will be placed close to your capital city.

Depending on the starting technology of your chosen tribe, you may be able to extract the resources on your first turn.

If you have access to resources on your first turn, and enough Stars to spend on extracting them it’s a good idea to upgrade your capital city on your first turn. 

Upgraded cities generate more income, can train more units, and benefit from other features – see the City upgrades section for more details. 

```
Note: You can only extract resources that fall within your city's territory, as defined by the border. 

Similarly, you can only build structures and monuments on territory that you control.
```


#### Research a new technology

You will have enough stars on your first turn to research a new technology. 

Depending on your surroundings, it may be essential to do this, if you’re surrounded by valuable resources that you can’t yet extract. 

Read the Technology section for a breakdown of the entire technology tree and an explanation of research costs.


#### Save your Stars

You don’t have to spend all or any of your , and in some situations, it might be better to hold off on spending until your next turn. 


### Starting currency

The number of Stars you have to spend at any one time are displayed at the top of the screen.

Above this is a counter, showing you how many **Stars Per Turn (SPT)** you’re currently earning. 

You spend Stars to extract **resources** (which you do in order to expand your city’s **Population**), research new **technologies**, or train new **units**. 


### Ending the first turn

Once you have made all of your available moves and purchases, and there’s nothing left for you to do, click the ‘End Turn’ button in the bottom right corner. 


### The next turn – exploring the map

Once all the other players have taken their turns, it’s your turn again. 

You will see that the amount of Stars you now have will have grown by however many SPT your capital is generating. 

You are free to move your starting unit again, as well as any unit that you trained in your capital city. 

Now you’re exploring, be on the lookout for nearby **Villages**, mysterious **Ruins**, and faraway **Lighthouses** – as well as other tribes. 


### Capture Villages

Villages are the most important features on a map after your capital city – make capturing and converting them to your tribe a priority. 

To capture a village, move a unit onto the Village tile. 

On the next turn, provided that the unit has not been destroyed, you will be given the option to capture the Village.

Capturing a Village turns it into a Level 1 city with a random name and a 3x3 border the same colour as your tribe’s colour, indicating that it is now part of your realm. 


### Explore Ruins

To explore a Ruin, you need to move a unit onto the tile, wait one turn and then select ‘Explore’ on the following turn, the same as if you were capturing a Village.

Exploring a Ruin will yield one of the following bonuses:

* **Resources:** ten Stars.
* **Explorer:** An Explorer unit leaves the Ruin, moves fifteen times, uncovering any tiles with fog-of-war as it goes.
* **Scrolls of Wisdom:** A free new technology from one of the available branches on the tech tree.
* **Population:** +3 Population Points for your capital city.
* **Veteran Swordsman:** A Veteran Swordsman (20HP) is spawned (Ruin must be on a land tile).
* **Rammer:** A Veteran Rammer with 15HP is spawned (Ruin must be on an ocean tile, Veteran Rammer will become a Veteran Warrior if moved on to a land tile).


### Discover Lighthouses

Every game features four **Lighthouses**, one located in each corner of the map. 

To discover a Lighthouse, you merely need to move a unit within sight range of the corner of the map. 

Discovering a Lighthouse gives you +1 Population Point in your capital city. 

Discovering all four Lighthouses lets you build the **Eye of God** Monument (+3 Population Points).


### City Levels and Population growth

On the first turn, you will have your capital city, which starts at **Level 1** and generates two SPT. 

To the right of the city’s name is a star symbol and a number, indicating how many SPT the city generates (in this case, a ‘2’). 

The crown symbol to the left to the city’s name indicates that it’s your capital. 

Other cities have no symbols to the left of their names. 

### Stars Per Turn (SPT)

The number of Stars Per Turn (SPT) that a city generates corresponds to its Level. 

A Level 1 city will generate 1 SPT, a Level 2 city will generate 2 STP, and so on. 

Your capital city benefits from a permanent +1 SPT bonus. Once your capital has grown to Level 4, it will generate 5 SPT, and once it has grown to Level 5, it will generate 6 SPT. 

When a city reaches Level 2, one of the **upgrades** you can unlock is the **Workshop** – this gives a city permanent +1 SPT bonus. 

The Workshop’s bonus stacks with a capital city’s natural +1 STP rate – a capital city with a workshop would therefore earn +2 STP. 

The Park upgrade also gives a city a +1 STP bonus, which stacks with both the Workshop, and your capital city’s +1 STP bonus. 


### How to earn more Stars 

* **Meet rival tribes** – When you meet a rival tribe for the first time, you will automatically earn 1 Star plus 2 Stars per 1000 points of the enemy's score (rounded up) for a maximum of 11 Stars.
* **Explore Ruins** – One of the possible outcomes for exploring Ruins is a one-off bonus of ten Stars.
* **Choose the ‘Resources’ upgrade when a city reaches Level 3** – When a city upgrades to Level 3, one of the upgrade options you can choose is ‘Resources’ a one-off bonus of five Stars.
* **Clear Forests** – Researching the **Forestry** technology gives you the **Clear Forests** ability, which removes the trees from **Forest** tiles, turning them into Field tiles, and earns you one extra Star.
* **Disband units** – Researching the **Free Spirit** technology gives you access to the Disband action, which lets you retire units from the map, and earn back half the amount of Stars (rounded up) you originally spent on them.
* **Harvest Starfish** – Researching the **Seafaring ** technology gives you access to the Gather Starfish action, which lets you extract starfish from ocean squares, which earns you eight Stars. 


### Population Progress Bar

Underneath every city’s name is a segmented progress bar. The number of segments represents how much of an **increase in Population** the city needs to grow to the next Level. 

As you grow the population of your city, the grey segments of the progress bar will turn blue. Once a progress bar is filled, the city will expand, and move up one Level. 

In the screengrab above, Chalicha, a Level 3 city, will need you to grow its Population by three more Points in order to become a Level 4 city. 

As a city’s Population grows, it **generates more SPT**, it can train and **support** more units, and it can gain **upgrades**, like city defences and expanded borders.

* Build structures
* Create Trade Routes to and from your capital city
* Build Temples
* Build Monuments
* Explore Ruins

#### Build structures

Other resources like **Lumber** and **Metal Deposits** cannot be extracted in the same way, but you can build specific structures – **Lumber Huts**, **Farms**, and **Mines** – that will provide Population Points:

* **Lumber Huts** can only be built on **Forest** tiles, and give you one Population Point. 
* **Farms** can only be built on **Field** tiles with **Crops** on them, and generate two Population Points.

## Combat

Eventually, as you explore the map, you will encounter enemy tribes. Initial encounters might not always be hostile – but they often are. 

There are two types of unit attacks, **melee** and **ranged**. 

Melee units – like **Warriors**, **Riders**, and **Swordsmen **– can attack enemy units in adjacent tiles. If the enemy unit survives the attack, it will **retaliate**.

Ranged units – like **Archers**, **Catapults**, and **Bombers** – can fire at enemy units from a distance indicated by the Range statistic. 

Ranged units will not receive retaliatory damage from melee units, unless they are occupying an adjacent tile. 

Most ranged units will retaliate if attacked by an adjacent melee unit or another ranged unit, assuming that they survived the attack. The only instance where a ranged unit will **not** offer retaliating fire is when the attacking unit is concealed by fog-of-war.

The Scout is a ranged naval unit that has an increased sight range, 

The **Bomber** is a ranged naval unit that has a special **splash** attack ability. This sees a projectile fired at a single unit which receives the brunt of the damage, but all adjacent units are also hit, or ‘splashed’, receiving smaller amounts of damage as well. Bombers also cannot retaliate. 


### How combat values work

Combat resolution in *Polytopia* is not random. It follows a specific formula, which you can use to work out exactly how much damage your units can expect to deal – and withstand – during encounters with the enemy. 

All units have two combat values for **attack** and **defence**, and all units have a number of ‘health points’ or ‘hit points’, abbreviated to **HP**. 

The attack value of a unit dictates how much attacking damage it can do, while the defence value reveals how much damage it can absorb. 

The overall amount of damage dealt and sustained is ultimately determined up a unit’s HP – a full strength unit will do significantly more damage than a unit that’s already lost most of its HP.

For example, the Warrior, the most basic unit of the game, has an attack value of 2, a defence value of 2, and 10HP. 

### The Polytopia combat formulas explained


#### Standard damage

The formulas for working out standard damage (which is the same for melee and ranged combat) is adapted from Mikael Mayer’s *Polytopia Calculator*, with additional notes:


```
defenceBonus = 1 (on open field tiles with no modifiers), 1.5 (in cities, or Forests, Mountains, Water, Ocean tiles with the appropriate technology researched), 4 (in cities with city walls)

attackForce = attacker.attack * (attacker.health / attacker.maxHealth)
defenceForce = defender.defence * (defender.health / defender.maxHealth) * defenceBonus 

totalDamage = attackForce + defenceForce 
attackResult = round((attackForce / totalDamage) * attacker.attack * 4.5) 
defenceResult = round((defenceForce / totalDamage) * defender.defence * 4.5)

Results are rounded to the nearest whole number. 

Retaliation damage is based on the HP value a defending unit has before combat begins, assuming that the defending unit survives.
```


**Example 1:** a full-strength Warrior (Attack: 2, Defence: 2, HP: 10) attacked another full-strength Warrior (Attack: 2, Defence: 2, HP: 10) on an open Field with no defence modifiers, here’s how that combat would resolve:

* *Warrior A’s attackForce: 2 multiplied by 1 (the attacker’s health (10) divided by the attacker’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1) = **2**.*
* *Warrior B’s defenceForce: 2 multiplied by 1 (the defender’s health (10) divided by the attacker’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1) = **2**.*

The value for totalDamage = attackForce (2) + defenceForce (2). In this case, the result would be **4**. 

Now, we take the attackForce (2) and divide that by the totalDamage (4), which gives us **0.5**. 

This is then multiplied by the attacker’s attack value (2), which gives us **1**. 

This is then multiplied by the accelerator value (4.5), which gives us **4.5**. 

Results are rounded to the nearest whole number, so this gives us a total of **5**. 

This means that Warrior A reduces Warrior B’s HP by 5, taking it down to **5HP**. 

Warrior B then retaliates. 

As the defending unit’s HP loss does not affect the retaliation result, Warrior B takes Warrior A down to **5HP** as well.

#### Splash damage

Attacks with splash damage are attacks which have an extra area-of-effect. This sees all units on tiles adjacent to the primary target take damage as well. 

The damage taken by the primary target is worked out in the exact same way as it would be if the unit was hit by a standard ranged attack. 

The damage taken by any surrounding units is worked out in the same way, but the result is then **divided by 2**, and the result is **rounded down to the nearest whole number**. 

Below is the formula for working out splash damage from projectiles, taken from the *Polytopia* wiki, with added comments: 

```
attackForce = attacker.attack * (attacker.health / attacker.maxHealth)
defenseForce = defender.defense * (defender.health / defender.maxHealth) * defenseBonus 
totalDamage = attackForce + defenseForce 

attackResult = round((attackForce / totalDamage) * attacker.attack * 4.5) 
attackSplash = attackResult / 2
```

Example: Bomber A attacks Rider B, which is based in a Forest. To the north of Rider B is Defender C, which sits in a city with City Walls, and therefore gets a defence bonus of 4.  

For the purposes of this example, Bomber A has 10HP, and is located out of melee range, so Rider B cannot retaliate. 

We’ll work out the damage inflicted on Rider B first. 


* *Bomber A’s attackForce: 3 multiplied by 1 (the attacker’s health (10) divided by the attacker’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1) = **3**.*
* *Rider B’s defenceForce: 1 multiplied by 1 (the defender’s health (10) divided by the defender’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1), multiplied by 1 = **1**.*

totalDamage = attackForce (3) + defenceForce (1) = **4**. 

– attackForce (3), divided by totalDamage (11) = **0.75**. 

– multiplied by the attack value (3) = **2.25**. 

– multiplied by the accelerator value (4.5) = **10.125.**

– rounded to the nearest whole number gives us a total of **10**.

Bomber A destroys Rider B. 

Calculating **splash damage** to Defender C in the adjacent city. 


* *Bomber A’s attackForce: 3 multiplied by 1 (the attacker’s health (10) divided by the attacker’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1) = **3**.*
* *Defender C’s defenceForce: 3 multiplied by 1 (the defender’s health (10) divided by the defender’s maximum health (also 10) equals 1), multiplied by 4 = **12**.*

totalDamage = attackForce (3) + defenceForce (12) = **15**. 

– attackForce (3), divided by totalDamage (11) = **0.2**. 

– multiplied by the attack value (3) = **0.6**. 

– multiplied by the accelerator value (4.5) = **2.7**

– rounded to the nearest whole number gives us a total of **3**.

As the splash damage is halved (to 1.5) and then rounded down, this inflicts **1HP** of damage on Defender C. 


### Healing

A damaged unit may choose to **heal** itself during its turn instead of moving or attacking. 

Healing sees a unit recover **4HP** if it’s based on home territory, or 2HP if based in neutral or enemy territory.

If you do nothing with a unit that has taken damage, it will **automatically heal itself** at the end of the turn.

The **Mind Bender** unit has the ‘Heal Others’ ability, which restores **4HP** to all adjacent friendly units. 

A unit cannot ‘recover’ HP beyond its natural maximum HP level. 


### Veteran status

After a unit kills **three** enemy units, it can be promoted to veteran status, which gives it an additional **5HP**, and restores the unit to full health. 

Note that super units cannot be promoted to veteran status, and kills made by naval units do not count towards gaining the veteran promotion.


### Zones of control

All units in Polytopia exert a **zone of control**, which means that if a unit moves onto a tile adjacent to a tile occupied by an enemy unit, that unit cannot move any further beyond those adjacent tiles, even if its Move statistic would normally allow it to.

Knowing how zones of control work is key to holding up enemy flanks, and preventing fast-moving units like **Riders** and **Knights** from dominating the map.

Destroying an enemy unit naturally removes the zone of control. 

Note that the **Cloak** unit ignores zones of control, whether it’s visible or invisible to the enemy.

* **Archer: **Inexpensive ranged unit, can move and fire. 
* **Swordsman:** Powerful melee unit, but not cheap to unlock or train.
* **Knight:** Fast, hard-hitting, unique **persist** ability means they can attack multiple times in one turn. Weak defence, expensive to unlock, high unit cost.
* **Catapult: **Highest attack in the game, but also the weakest defence. Cannot move and fire in the same turn. 
* **Mind Bender:** Can heal friendly units and convert enemy units to their side. Cannot move and heal or convert in the same turn, and cannot retaliate.
* **Cloak:** Turns invisible when it moves across the map, and ignores terrain movement penalties. If a Cloak attacks an enemy city, it will incite a revolt, which sees a number of **Dagger** or **Pirate** units spawned. 

    Any units inside the city will be hit with an attack force of **2**. If the city is unoccupied when it is attacked by a **Cloak**, a **Dagger** unit will immediately spawn on the city’s tile, and will begin capturing it. The city will also produce no Stars on the opponent’s next turn


    **Cloaks** will be discovered if an enemy unit tries to move onto the same tile it occupies, and can be attacked. **Cloaks** are not immune to splash damage. **Cloaks** cannot attack enemy units, they can only attack cities. 

* **Dagger/Pirate:** **Daggers** have stats equivalent to that of a **Warrior**, but have the unique **surprise **ability, which prevents an attacked unit from retaliating. They cannot be trained, and are only created when a **Cloak** attacks a city. 
* The number of **Daggers** spawned is the same as the infiltrated city’s Level (up to a maximum of five). A **Pirate** is essentially a seaborne equivalent of a **Dagger**. **Daggers **and **Pirates **cannot be upgraded to Veteran status. 
* **Giant:** A large, powerful unit with the best stats in the game. Cannot be trained, only chosen as the **Super Unit** upgrade when a city reaches Levels 5 and beyond. **Giants **cannot be upgraded to Veteran status.

Unlike land-based units, naval units cannot be trained in cities; they must be created at **ports** (which cost seven Stars and require the **Fishing** technology to build).

Naval units are created by moving a land unit onto a **Water** tile that has had a **Port** built on it. 

Note that the **HP** of all naval units is determined by the HP of the land unit that ‘creates’ it.

For example, a Veteran Defender with 20HP can therefore be turned into a naval unit with 20HP, a regular Swordsman with 15HP can become a naval unit with 15HP, while a damaged Warrior with 7HP would become a damaged naval unit with 7HP, and so on. 

The exception to this is the **Pirate** – the water-borne version of the **Dagger** – which always starts with 10HP. 

In combat, the attack and defence force of naval units are worked out in the exact same way as it is for land units. 

Naval units can heal and be healed in the same way as land units. 


#### Creating naval units

When you move a land unit onto a port, the unit will be immediately converted into a **Raft**. 

A unit on a **Raft** can move around on **Water** tiles. **Rafts** have a defence value of 2, but cannot attack other units, nor can they retaliate – they’re essentially transport ships.

By spending Stars, you can upgrade units on **Rafts** to one of three vessel types:


* **Rammer:** A sea-bourne melee unit. As the name implies, it can attack other units by ramming into them. It can retaliate, but only if rammed by another Rammer, or attacked by an adjacent land unit. 
* **Scout:** A fast-moving vessel with a ranged attack that can move and fire in the same turn, and can retaliate in the same way that an **Archer** would.
* **Bomber:** A battleship with a powerful ranged attack that benefits from splash damage, which damages the target, and any adjacent enemy units. Bombers cannot move and fire in the same turn, and they cannot retaliate. 

**Giants** moved onto a port will become **Juggernauts**. These are melee naval units, and attack enemy ships in the same way that **Rammers** do.

Whenever **Juggernauts** move or attack enemy units, they also damage adjacent enemy units, in the same way that the **Bomber’s** splash area-of-effect does.


## Peace Treaties and Embassies

The **Strategy** and **Diplomacy** technologies lets you sign **Peace Treaties** with rival tribes, and build Embassies in their capital cities.


### Signing a Peace Treaty

Signing a **Peace Treaty** with another tribe or player requires them to accept the treaty – and vice versa. 

A computer-controlled player will not accept an offering of a Peace Treaty from you if you have consistently attacked them in the past, or have moved units into territory controlled by them. You can improve your relationship with a computer-controlled player by ceasing attacks and moving units away from their cities. 

If both sides agree to a Peace Treaty, this will generate an extra **2 SPT** for both tribes. 


### Building an Embassy

Building an Embassy costs five Stars, and if you build an Embassy in the capital city of a tribe you’ve signed a Peace Treaty with, both tribes get **4 SPT** instead. 

While two tribes are at peace, they cannot attack each other’s units, and do not exert a zone of control. 

**Cloak** units, which would normally be invisible, can also be seen by both players. 


### Breaking a Peace Treaty

Peace Treaties can be broken at any time, but the player who wants to break the treaty cannot attack the other player on that turn, and any units within the other player’s borders will be forcibly Disbanded. 


## Technology

Researching new Technologies lets you create new units, new buildings, exploit new resources, and perform new actions. 

The Technology tree has five main branches that fork once, creating **three Tiers**. 

At the start of the game, Tier 1 technologies cost five Stars, Tier 2s cost six Stars, and Tier 3s cost seven Stars to research.  

For **each additional city that joins your tribe**, technology research costs **increase** by one, two, or three Stars for Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 technologies, respectively.

### Disbanding units 

Researching the **Free Spirit** technology gives you access to the Disband action, which lets you retire units from the map, and earn back half the amount of Stars (rounded up) you originally spent on them. 

Reasons for wanting to Disband a unit include:

* Your cities cannot support any more units 
* You want to deny your rivals the opportunity to destroy your unit(s) 
* You need some extra Stars 

### Destroying buildings

Researching the **Chivalry** technology gives you access to the **Destroy** action. 

This lets you remove structures like Sawmills, Windmills, Forges and Markets if you realise later on that these structures could sit in a more advantageous place on the map. 

Destroy is free to use, but unlike the similar Disband ability, using Destroy does not return any Stars to the player.

Using Destroy on a structure immediately removes any Population Points or Stars generated by the structure. It is therefore possible for a city to slip into negative Population, potentially reducing its STP to zero. 

This aspect of Destroy can be used offensively, in 'scorched earth' manner; in a situation where a city you control is about to be captured. Using Destroy on everything before capture denies your opponent a lucrative prize, and requires them to spend Stars on rebuilding.


### Expanding and rebuilding Lumber Huts and Farms

You can use the **Destroy** action in tandem with the **Grow Forest** and **Burn Forest** actions to create more **Lumber Huts** and **Farms** for your cities. 

This greatly improves the output of **Sawmills** and **Windmills**, and has the potential to upgrade your cities up several levels. 

Because expanding and rebuilding farms costs a lot of Stars, this strategy is best deployed in Domination and Might games, when you’ll be taking lots of turns, and can guarantee a return on your investment. 

It costs five Stars to use Grow Forest, which can only be performed on an empty Field tile. 

From there you can either build a Lumber Hut (three Stars) or use Burn Forest (five Stars) to turn the Forest tile into Crops, which can then be turned into a Farm (five Stars). 

It therefore costs a total of **eight Stars** to turn a Field tile into a Forest tile with a Lumber Hut, which gives you one Population Point, plus another, if it’s built next to a Sawmill. 

Building a Farm from scratch costs **fifteen Stars**, which nets you two Population Points, and another if the Farm is built next to a Windmill.

## Point scoring explained

In **Perfection** and **Glory** games, the winner is either whoever amasses the most points after 30 turns, or whoever earns a set amount of points first. 

Note that points are still calculated in **Domination** and **Might **games, but they have no bearing on victory conditions. 

Points are earned for performing the following actions, grouped into the following categories:


### Army & Territory

* **Units:** 5 points per Star of a unit’s cost. Training a Warrior (cost: two Stars) would therefore earn you 10 points. You gain no points for naval unit upgrades, converted enemy units, nor do you lose points for disbanding units. 
* **Super Units:** 50 points per Super Unit created. 
* **Territory:** 20 points per tile covered by your city’s territory. 
* **Exploration:** 5 points per map tile uncovered. 


### Cities

* **Cities:** 100 points per city controlled, plus 50 points per Level above Level 1.
* **Park:** 250 points per Park upgrade.


### Monuments and Temples

* **Monuments:** 400 points each.
* **Temples:** 100 points, plus an extra 50 points every two turns (for a max. of 300 points)


### Science


* **Technology:** 100 points per tier of the technology.

# Summary of player’s guide

Winning at *Polytopia *requires you to pay attention to two layers of action – your units and your cities.

You need a strong economy to train and support a large army, but you also need a large army to defend and support a strong economy.

Understanding how these two layers of action relate is key to winning games against skilled opponents. 

While the strategy you’ll take every time you play depends on your starting terrain, starting technology and available resources, here are some general tips to keep in mind:


* Explore early, explore often – *see Removing fog-of-war.*
* Grow your capital city to *at least* Level 3 at the earliest opportunity – *see How to grow your city’s Population.*
* Don’t expand your capital’s borders until you know where the surrounding Villages – which you should turn into your Cities – are located – *see Actions after moving your first unit.*
* Lighthouses will always be sitting in the corners, go to the nearest one as soon as you can – *see Discover Lighthouses*.
* Investing in technology early pays off in the long run –* see Technology*.
* Make sure you research Philosophy early. The 33% technology research discount becomes less useful as the game progresses – *see Tier 3 Technologies*.
* As soon as you can support more units, train them – *see Lists of units*.
* Warriors are fine to begin with, but as soon as you can afford to regularly train Swordsmen, don’t go back to Warriors – *see Lists of units*.
* Similarly, Defenders are great; cheap, flexible, can hold up an enemy flank, but Swordsmen are just as good on the defence, and they can move and attack in the same turn. Defenders can’t – *see Lists of units*.
* Catapults have a great range and high attack, but weak defence. One Knight will go through a line of catapults like a knife through butter – *see Lists of units*.
* Any unit can capture a city, even if it’s on 1HP  – *see Lists of units*.
* Ruins are great – especially if they give you a population boost – but don’t prioritise them over Villages – *see Explore Ruins*.
* Don’t put down a Monument until you are 100% sure where it’s going to sit – *see Monuments and Temples*. 
* Finally, be flexible – if conditions aren’t optimal, make do.
